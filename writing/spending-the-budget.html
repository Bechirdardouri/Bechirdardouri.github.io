Spending
the Budget

The architecture decisions that actually move the needle.

Bechir DardouriJuly 202618 min read

If you read enough model reports back to back, a strange thing happens: the architectures start to blur together.

Decoder-only transformer, RMSNorm, rotary positions, SwiGLU, grouped-query attention, a mixture-of-experts feed-forward. DeepSeek, Qwen, Gemma, Llama, gpt-oss, Kimi: put their block diagrams side by side and you are mostly looking at the same picture with the labels swapped.

So where does the actual engineering happen?

It happens in the parts that do not show up in the block diagram. It happens in how the budget is spent. Every model is built against a fixed ceiling: a parameter count you can afford to store, a FLOP count you can afford per token, and a KV cache you can afford to keep in memory during inference. Almost every decision that separates a good small model from a mediocre one is a decision about where to spend those fixed resources—and where to refuse to spend them.

There is an honest caveat to all of this. Almost none of what follows was derived. It was found. The field tries a thing at small scale, watches the loss curve, and keeps the thing if the curve goes down. Theory, when it comes at all, often arrives years later as a retrofit.

The block diagram converged years ago. The budgeting is where models still differ.

That is the lens for this essay. No new attention variants to memorize; instead, a set of quiet decisions about embeddings, attention softmaxes, experts, routing, positional information, and KV memory. Individually they look like footnotes. Together they are most of the difference.

01The embedding table is the first place small models bleed

The very first and last thing a language model does is convert between token IDs and vectors. An embedding matrix maps each of V vocabulary entries to a dmodel-dimensional vector; an unembedding matrix maps the final hidden state back to V logits. Both have shape V × dmodel. For a large vocabulary, that is a lot of parameters sitting at the two ends of the network.

The first decision is whether those two matrices should be the same matrix. Tying embeddings—using the input embedding, transposed, as the output projection—is one of the oldest tricks in the book. The table is a fixed cost that does not scale with depth. In a 7B model it is a rounding error; in a 400M model with a 128k-token vocabulary it can consume a quarter of the entire parameter budget.

Tying immediately hands that slice back. At equal quality, ablations tend to find tied models no worse, sometimes better, than untied models. And the recent small-model answer to what to buy with those reclaimed parameters is simple: buy depth. At an equivalent parameter budget, more layers tends to beat untying the embeddings.

There is a second, subtler decision hiding in the same table: do not apply weight decay to it. Dense matrices in the middle of the network participate in every forward pass; the embedding table is different. Each row is only touched when its token appears, so decay pushes rare-token embeddings toward zero much more aggressively than common ones. OLMo 2 and the SmolLM family simply exclude embeddings from weight decay. The reported quality does not move, but embedding norms become calmer—and calmer norms make a run less likely to diverge.

Finally, make V a friendly number. Rounding the vocabulary up to a multiple of 64, 128, or 256 can unlock faster matmul kernels. The GPU does not care that a few extra rows are unused; it cares that the language-model head tiles cleanly. Spending a few thousand dead vocabulary entries for a faster kernel is one of the better trades in the whole model.

TakeawayFor small models, the embedding table is not a detail. Tie it, stop decaying it, size it for the hardware—then spend the savings on depth.

02Attention has to look somewhere. Give it a nowhere.

Softmax attention is a forced choice: the weights over the context must sum to one. That works when there is genuinely something to attend to. But a head sometimes has nothing relevant to look at: its specialized pattern simply is not present in this sequence. The head would like to output nothing. Softmax will not allow it, so probability mass gets smeared across the context and noise enters the residual stream.

Models discovered a workaround before anyone designed one. StreamingLLM observed that transformers dump excess attention mass onto the first few tokens: positions visible to essentially every later token and therefore convenient to over-attend to. These attention sinks act as a drain. This is why naive sliding-window generation can collapse when it evicts the first tokens: it has thrown away the model's drain.

The elegant repair is to stop hijacking real tokens and give every head an explicit place to attend to nothing. gpt-oss does exactly this: a single learned scalar per head is added to the denominator of the softmax.

αi = exp(q · ki) / (Σj exp(q · kj) + exp(sinkh))

When all real scores are low, the head can route most of its mass into sinkh and effectively pay attention to no token at all. It is Miller's off-by-one softmax with the constant made learnable and per-head. One parameter per head removes an instability models were otherwise papering over by abusing their own first tokens.

Vision found the same principle independently. In DINOv2 feature maps, ViT models turned blank-sky or flat-wall patches into high-norm outliers: the model had gone looking for scratch space, found none, and seized a piece of the image. Vision Transformers Need Registers fixes this by appending a few learnable tokens that correspond to no image region, letting them participate in attention, and discarding them at the output.

TakeawayA softmax that must sum to one cannot express “none of the above.” Give the model an explicit nowhere and it stops manufacturing one out of real data.

03Mixture-of-experts: pretend to be huge, act small

For most frontier-scale models, the feed-forward layer is no longer a single dense MLP. It is a mixture of experts. You store many expert MLPs but activate only a few per token. A model can carry 600B+ parameters and touch roughly 37B of them on any token. Stored parameters give capacity; activated parameters set the FLOP bill.

Once you commit to sparsity, two questions follow. First: how sparse? More total experts at a fixed number of active experts reliably lowers training and validation loss. Under a fixed active-parameter budget, increasing total experts is close to a free lunch until system and load-balancing costs dominate.

Second: how big should each expert be? Let the dense feed-forward width be dintermediate = 4dmodel. Granularity G asks how many small experts add up to that dense width.

dintermediate = 4dmodel = G · dexpert

Higher G means many thin experts; lower G means a few fat ones. DeepSeekMoE's finding is intuitive: grow expert count by a factor m, shrink each expert to 1/m of a standard FFN, and activate m times as many. FLOPs remain fixed while knowledge can be carved up more finely. Finer granularity helps, with diminishing returns, while routing and kernel overhead grow.

One other structural choice has quietly become standard: the shared expert. Alongside routed experts, retain one or a few that every token passes through. Common patterns no longer need to be independently rediscovered by many specialists. The shared expert handles the baseline; the routed experts can specialize in what actually differs.

TakeawaySparsity buys capacity without buying FLOPs. Granularity controls how finely that capacity is carved up, while a shared expert absorbs the common case.

04The router is the hard part

Everything above assumes the router sends tokens to the right experts in reasonable proportions. In practice, that is where MoE training goes to die. Left alone, a router can funnel most tokens into a small handful of experts. Those experts get all the gradient, improve, attract more tokens, and the rest of the expensively stored model sits idle. This routing collapse is bad for quality and bad for hardware utilization.

The traditional fix is an auxiliary load-balancing loss. It works, but it fights the model: balancing is a second objective bolted onto language modeling. Turn it up enough to matter and it distorts the gradient; you end up trading model performance for hardware utilization.

DeepSeek-V3 instead uses auxiliary-loss-free balancing. Give each expert a scalar bias. The bias is added only for the top-k selection decision—who gets picked—then removed, so the actual gating weights use the original unbiased scores. Between steps, overloaded experts have their bias nudged down and underloaded experts have it nudged up. Load balances without ever polluting the language-modeling gradient.

Separate who to route to from how much to weight them. Balance the former; leave the latter to the model.

The earliest layers are also special. Recent MoE models commonly keep the first one to three feed-forward layers dense. Early hidden states have absorbed little context; a learned router can do little more than a near-static lexical assignment, and forcing it to route here invites collapse. If you must route early, HashMoE offers an alternative: use a fixed hash of the token for a balanced deterministic assignment.

TakeawayThe experts are the easy part. Balance the router outside the loss, and treat early layers as a special zone where dense or deterministic routing beats learned routing.

05The scaling factor nobody explains

When outputs from the top-k experts are combined, you take a weighted average of vectors. Averaging has an easy-to-forget consequence: if experts point in different directions—the whole point of having specialists—their coordinates partially cancel. The routed branch comes out weaker than a dense FFN would.

Let each active expert output RMS r and let the routing weights be nearly uniform, gi ≈ 1/k. If expert outputs are roughly uncorrelated, the combined branch has magnitude:

‖ Σ giEi(x) ‖rms ≈ r / √k

With k = 8, the signal is only about 1/√8 ≈ 0.35 of a single expert's magnitude. The repair is equally simple: multiply the combination by a routed scaling factor near √k. That is why top-8 designs often land near 2.827 and top-6 ones around 2.5. The exact value is tuned, not dictated, but the √k intuition tells you what the mysterious constant is for.

TakeawayAveraging independent expert vectors shrinks them by about √k. The routed scaling factor exists to undo that shrinkage.

06RoPE, NoPE, and a spontaneous division of labor

Attention alone is order-blind, so models need positional information. For years the standard answer was rotary position embeddings, or RoPE: rotate query and key vectors by an amount determined by position. RoPE works well, but it carries a recency bias. Nearby tokens are easier to attend to—great for local coherence, less great for retrieving one fact from far back.

The interesting finding is that you do not have to choose. Remove positional encoding from some layers entirely—NoPE—and interleave them with ordinary RoPE layers. The network spontaneously divides the labor: RoPE layers aggregate local information; NoPE layers, freed from positional pull, specialize in long-range retrieval. Llama 4's interleaved iRoPE is a compelling example of gaining a capability not by adding machinery, but by removing it from the right layers.

There is a second route to the same destination. If an architecture already carries position implicitly, as Mamba-style state-space layers do, attention layers may not need RoPE at all. A model that never used RoPE cannot suffer RoPE's out-of-distribution behaviour when its context window is extended later.

TakeawayPosition is not all or nothing. Interleave RoPE and NoPE for a retrieval-versus-recency division of labor, or use implicit position to avoid RoPE extension problems entirely.

07KV sharing: the cheapest long-context win

The final budget line is memory. During generation, every layer stores a key and value vector for every token it has seen. That KV cache avoids recomputation, but at long context it is often the resource that actually runs out.

Grouped-query attention shares keys and values across heads within a layer. Cross-layer KV sharing attacks redundancy along the other axis: nearby layers' key/value projections are highly redundant, so later layers can reuse the KV tensors computed by an earlier layer instead of computing and storing their own.

Gemma 3n makes the idea concrete. In its E2B configuration, only the first block of layers computes its own key/value projections; later layers reuse KV from the most recent earlier layer of the same attention type. The reported result is a much smaller cache with little cost to quality. On constrained hardware, this is one of the highest-leverage decisions available because it shrinks the resource that decides whether a long context fits at all.

TakeawayKV cache is the inference budget that bites in long-context deployment. Sharing across layers—not only heads—reclaims a large chunk of it for little quality cost.

∗The thread that ties it together

Step back and the same pattern repeats:

  • tie embeddings and buy depth with the savings;
  • stop decaying the embedding table and size the vocabulary for hardware;
  • give each attention head a legitimate way to attend to nothing;
  • go sparse to separate capacity from cost, then use granularity and shared experts well;
  • balance routing outside the loss and protect early layers from learned routing;
  • rescale routed outputs by about √k;
  • interleave RoPE and NoPE, then share KV across layers to shrink the inference footprint.

Not one of these is a new kind of layer. Every one is a choice about where to spend parameters, FLOPs, gradient signal, or cache memory—and, as often, where to refuse to spend. That is what modern architecture work is once you get past the block diagram. The blocks converged years ago. The budgeting is where the good models earn it.
